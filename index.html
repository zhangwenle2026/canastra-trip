CHAPTER 01 · ROTEIRO A Travessia

Four days, two thousand kilometres of asphalt and gravel, one national park where the longest river in Brazil is born from a crack in the rock. The Serra da Canastra is not a place you stumble upon; it is a pilgrimage for those who crave silence, altitude, and the honest taste of artisanal cheese aged in mountain air.

Our route begins in the haze of São Paulo, threading northwest through sugarcane country until the cerrado opens up, red and gold under a winter sun. The destination: a plateau at 1,400 metres where the sky feels close enough to touch, and the only sound is wind bending the canela-de-ema grass.

The São Francisco does not begin with grandeur. It seeps from the earth, quiet and cold, as if unsure of the 2,914 kilometres ahead.

This is a road trip for slow travellers. Expect dirt roads that demand patience, cheese that demands a second slice, and sunsets that demand you put down your phone. The GWM H6 will carry us through it all, its AWD whispering over gravel while we watch for maned wolves in the golden grass.

CHAPTER 02 · DESTAQUES Six Encounters

Every landscape has its landmarks, but Canastra offers something rarer: encounters that engage all senses. The thunder of falling water, the sharp tang of aged cheese, the warmth of volcanic-soil coffee on your tongue. Here are six moments you will carry home.

01
Casca d'Anta

At 186 metres, this is where the São Francisco River makes its first dramatic statement. The upper viewpoint offers a panorama of the falls framed by cerrado canopy; the lower trail (moderate, 3km return) takes you to the misty base where rainbow arcs form in the spray. Come at dawn for solitude and the best light.

02
Chapadão da Canastra

A 1,400-metre plateau of grassland and rocky outcrops, crossed by a gravel road that stretches to the horizon. The cerrado ecosystem here shelters endemic orchids, crystal-clear streams, and some of Brazil's rarest mammals. Drive slowly, windows down, and let the landscape unfold at its own pace.

03
Queijo Canastra

Two centuries of tradition in every wheel. The pingo culture (natural whey starter), raw milk, and mountain aging caves give this IPHAN-heritage cheese its unmistakable tang. Visit a family fazenda to see the morning milking, the hand-pressing, and the curing rooms. Buy the meia-cura (half-aged) for the road.

04
Complexo do Claro

A staircase of waterfalls carved into red sandstone, creating natural pools of crystal-clear water connected by rock slides polished smooth by millennia. The complex includes multiple falls of varying heights, each with its own swimming hole. The water is refreshingly cold even in winter.

05
Lago de Furnas

Known as the "Sea of Minas," this 1,440km² reservoir creates a dramatic landscape of fjord-like inlets, golden sunsets, and lakeside communities. At Delfinópolis, the lake meets the mountains, offering kayaking, fishing, and evening light that turns the water to molten copper.

06
Café Mineiro

Minas Gerais produces some of Brazil's finest specialty coffees, and the volcanic soil around Canastra adds mineral complexity to every cup. Visit a micro-lot farm to trace the journey from cherry to cup: hand-picking, natural drying on raised beds, and roasting that reveals notes of chocolate, citrus, and brown sugar.

CHAPTER 03 · NOTAS DO VIAJANTE Before You Go

Planning a Canastra road trip requires less logistics than you might expect, but a few practical details will make the difference between a good trip and a great one. Here is what we learned.

☀
Best Season
May to September (dry season). Clear skies, cool nights (8-18C), best waterfall flow. July is peak but manageable on weekdays.
⚙
Vehicle
High-clearance SUV recommended. Park roads are gravel/dirt. 4WD not mandatory in dry season but helpful. Fuel up before entering the park.
☂
What to Wear
Layers for cold mornings and warm afternoons. Hiking boots for trails, sandals for waterfalls. Sunscreen essential at altitude.
★
Connectivity
Cell signal is weak to nonexistent in the park. Download offline maps. Pousadas have WiFi. Embrace the disconnection.
☕
Food and Water
No restaurants inside the park. Pack a cooler with snacks, water, and lunch. Towns have simple but excellent comida mineira.
⛩
Park Entry
R$36 per person (2024 price). Open 8am-6pm (last entry 4pm). Casca d'Anta sector closes earlier. Check ICMBio website.
